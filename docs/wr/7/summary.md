# Financing and Trading

## Business Financing

**equity** financing:

- provided by the owners (&rarr; shareholders)
- investors bring know-how and networks
- no repayment obligation[^1]
- investors participate in profits
- investors (usually) have voting rights
- negotiation / administrative procedures take time

[^1]: The company doesn't _need_ to pay back anything

**debt** financing:

- capital comes from external creditors _(e.g. bank)_
- available in large amounts
- leverage possible
- interest reduces taxable profit
- no sharing of voting rights or profits
- lenders may impose restrictions
- regular interest payments
- repayment obligation
- insolvency risk

**asset liquidation**:

- raising capital by selling unused fixed assets
- rapid liquidity generation
- no interest or repayment obligations
- loss of company assets
- potential long-term disadvantage

**self-financing**:

- profits are retained in company
- independence from debt + no additional costs
- slow process (profit-dependent)
- lack of immediate capital limits growth

## Loans

**personal guarantee**:

- another person promises to pay back if the primary borrower can't do it
- doesn't require collateral from the borrower, as the guarantor's personal assets are on the line

**collateral**:

- borrower forfeits predefined assets _(collateral)_ on failure to repay the loan
- lender can seize the assets as collateral to recover the debt

**unsecured** loan:

- borrower doesn't provide collateral
- approval based on creditworthiness
- borrower usually provides financial history, income etc.

**lombard** loan:

- secured loan
- movable property as collateral _(stocks, bonds, precious metals, life insurance, cars etc.)_
- borrower loses access to collateral on failure to repay

**mortgage**:

- secured loan
- immovable property (real estate) as collateral _(house, land, appartment etc.)_
- lender can foreclose the borrower's property if they can't pay

## Balance Sheet

**profitability**:

- profit in relation to **invested capital**
- high profits usually mean higher risk and/or lower liquidity

!!! info "Return On Equity"
    - measures **profit** in relation to the **equity used** (in %)
    - higher value means more profitable
    - should be **higher** than market interest rate

    $$\text{ROE} = \frac{\text{Profit}}{\text{Equity}} \cdot 100$$

**liquidity**:

- ensures short-term obligations can be met
- quickly available funds make up liquidity

!!! info "Liquidity Ratio 2"
    - share of short-term liabilities that can currently be paid (in %)
    - should be minimum 100%
    - liquid funds: cash and bank account

    $$\text{Liquidity Ratio 2} = \frac{\text{Liquid Funds + Accounts Receivable}}{\text{Short-term Liabilities}} \cdot 100$$

**risk**:

- investor aims to minimise risk &rarr; protect capital from losses
- companies should aim to get money with **as little restrictions as possible**:
    - preferrably no decision-making rights to third parties (like in stocks)
    - favourable financing duration for loans
    - low interest rate

!!! info "Equity Ratio / Debt Ratio"
    - measures distribution of financing methods of a company
    - higher equity ratio &rarr; generally more stable / secure funding
    - $\text{Debt Ratio} + \text{Equity Ratio} = 100\%$
    - **debt** ratio should be **below 70%**
    - **equity** ratio should be **above 30%**

    $$\text{Debt Ratio} = \frac{\text{Debt Capital}}{\text{Total Capital}} \cdot 100$$

    $$\text{Equity Ratio} = \frac{\text{Equity}}{\text{Total Capital}} \cdot 100$$

## Leverage

- use borrowed money in addition to your investments
- amplifies both gains and losses
- if return on investment exceeds interest rate on debts &rarr; profits
- strongly increases risk

## Asset Classes

### Cash

- cash equivalts preserve capital with instant conversion
- loses purchasing power over time (inflation)
- holding too much cash guarantees loss of purchasing power over time

### Commodities

- raw materials
- no cashflow generation
- profits only through appreciation
- good inflation protection
- gold = hedge against currency devaluation
- volatile &rarr; risky
- ETFs are more common

### Real Estate

- buying physical property
- rental income &rarr; tenants pay rent
- appreciation
- not liquid &rarr; unsuitable for quick cash
- lower returns than stocks, but still high
- REIT Real Estate Investment Trusts

### Bonds

- lending money to state or corporation
- regular interest payments
- considered lower-risk than stocks
- legal claim to your money &rarr; not dependent on company success
- corporate bonds more likely to default (6% vs 3%) &rarr; higher return rates
- ca. 3-4% yearly returns

### Stocks

- piece of stock = share of company
- direct ownership of a piece of the company
- if company makes profits &rarr; stock gains in value
- dividends may be paid out (quarterly) directly from company profits
- volatile &rarr; high-risk, high-return
- ca. 9-10% yearly returns

## Equity and Bonds

| stocks | bonds |
|:-|:-|
| issued by companies to raise capital from public | issued primarily by e.g. governments |
| grant the buyer partial ownership of the company | don't come with voting rights |
| profits through appreciation and potentially dividends | lender gets paid interest regularly |
| no guaranteed returns | guaranteed returns |
| higher risk, higher rewards | lower risk, lower but constant rewards |
| stockholders hold stocks | bondholders hold bonds |
| stockholders = **owners** of the firm | bondholders = **lenders** to the firm |
| company goes bankrupt &rarr; money gone | company goes bankrupt &rarr; debt paid back if possible |

- bondholders are prioritised in payback if the company goes bankrupt
- **preference** stockholders get paid first if the company goes bankrupt
- **equity** stockholders get paid with what remains afterwards

## Stock market

**stock exchange**:

- ensures price transparency
- intermediary between supply and demand
- ensures trade is conducted efficiently
- regulates trading activity
- provides price information
- **publicity transformation**: offer IPOs[^2] for companies that go public
- functions as "economic barometer" &rarr; provides a good estimate of how the economy is doing

[^2]: **I**nitial **P**ublic **O**ffering

**market indexes**:

- represent a portfolio of investment holdings
- benchmarks to gauge movement / performance of market segments
- can be traded as ETFs[^3]

[^3]: **E**xchange **T**raded **F**unds

## Futures and Options

Both futures and options provide the buyer with the right or obligation to **buy something at a later date for a predetermined price**, though they differ in some aspects:

| futures | options |
|:-|:-|
| buyer is **obligated** to buy at determined price | buyer reserves the **right** to buy at predetermined price |
| buyer is **obligated** to buy at determined date | buyer can buy **before or at expiry** |
| no premium is paid | extra fee (premium) is paid by the buyer |
| maximum loss of 100% | maximum loss of the value of the fee |

!!! tip "Core Trade-off Between Futures and Options"
    Futures are a cheaper, clearer instrument but you "need to be right", else you can lose a lot of money. Options give you protection and flexibility, but you pay a (usually substantial) fee for that luxury.

??? info "Futures and Margins"
    > We don't need to know this for the exam, but I found it helped my understanding of futures, so I included it here as well.

    Since futures obligate both parties (buyer and seller), a **margin system** acts as collateral to ensure both parties can fulfill their contractual obligations:

    - **initial margin**: deposit (ca. 3-12% of contract value) that acts as collateral; returned when closing the position
    - **maintenance margin**: minimum balance to be maintained while position is open; if balance falls below margin &rarr; margin call
    - **margin call**: trader must top up the account balance or the position is forcibly closed
    - **mark-to-market**: gains / losses are applied to the account _every day_ based on closing price &rarr; losses are realised immediately, not at expiry

    These factors make futures a high-risk asset.
