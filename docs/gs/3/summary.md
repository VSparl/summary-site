# Russian Revolution

## Situation Around 1900
- world's largest country in area comprising &#8537; of the earth's surface
- multi-national empire with over 100 nationalities
- less than half of the population ethnically Russian
- **Russification**: policy forcing Russian language and culture on minorities like Poles and Finns
- difficult communication due to sheer size
- absolute autocracy ruled by Nicholas II (1894&ndash;1917)
- no parliament and no accountability to the people
- regime supported by the **Okhrana** (secret police), large army, and orthodox church
- inefficient bureaucracy with limited effectiveness

## Social and Economic Problems
- over 80% of the population made up of peasants
- serfs emancipated by Alexander II in 1861 but burdened by **redemption payments**[^1] for 49 years
- land held collectively by the **mir** (commune) which ferquently redistributed plots
- rapid population growth led to hunger and poverty
- industrialisation promoted by finance minister **Sergei Witte** in the 1890s
- massive railway expansion including the ~6'500 km **trans-siberian railway**
- growing proletariat in cities facing bad living and working conditions

[^1]: payments for the land they received

## Political Parties and Factions
- **Narodniks**: saw peasantry as revolutionary class; revolution would lead directly to socialism
- **Monarchists**: supported the Tsar and opposed land expropriation[^2]
- **Octobrists**: moderate liberals supporting the 1905 constitutional changes
- **Cadets**: radical liberals seeking a modern constitutional state
- **Trudoviks**: wanted land for the peasantry and economic support from small businesses
- **Social Revolutionaries (SR)**: populist[^3] party focused on the peasantry
- **Bolsheviks**: led by Lenin; wanted a classless society
- **Mensheviks**: moderate wing believing socialism required a prior period of capitalist development

## 1905 Revolution
- long-term causes:
    - social inequality
    - lack of political reform
    - peasant poverty
    - stable, autocratic system built upon dynamic society
    - societal changes due to industrialisation
- short-term causes:
    - defeats in the **Russo-Japanese war** (1904&ndash;1905)
    - food & heating is not readily available in winter
- **Bloody Sunday**: massacre of peaceful protestors leads to nationwide strikes
- **October Manifesto**: issued by the Tsar to survive the uprising
- promised civil liberties, freedom of speech, and an elected **Duma** (parliament)
- no new laws can be introduced without Duma approval

## Effects of 1905
- Tsar kept power because opponents were not united and the army remained loyal
- Nicholas II ignored manifesto promises and limited the Duma's power
- **Peter Stolypin**: prime minister (1906&ndash;1911) who used both repression and reform
- thousands executed to crush revolutionaries
- land reforms encouraged peasants to buy land and become **Kulaks** (prosperous farmers)
- assassination of Stolypin in 1911 removed an able minister
- growth of scandals involving the holy man **Rasputin** discredited the royal family

## 1917 Revolutions
**February** revolution:

- spontaneous uprising caused by WWI failures and bread shortages
- mutiny of the Petrograd[^4] military base led to the Tsar's abdication
- establishment of a **provisional government** alongside the **Petrograd Soviet** (dual power)

[^4]: was later called _Leningrad_ and is now called _Saint Petersburg_

**October** revolution:

- well-organised Bolshevik coup led by Lenin and Trotsky
- provisional government overthrown for continuing the war and failing land reform
- Bolsheviks promised "peace, bread, land" to secure support

## Consolidation of Power (1918&ndash;1924)
> We probably don't need to know this for the test

- **Russian civil war**: reds (Bolsheviks) vs whites (Monarchists, liberals, other socialists)
- Bolshevik victory facilitated by red army leadership under Trotsky
- party renamed to the **communist party**
- **Lenin's death**: passed away in January 1924 after consolidating the new state

## Historical Interpretations
> We probably don't need to know this for the test

- **Soviet view**: inevitable triumph of the workers led by a scientific marxist party
- **unfinished revolution**: genuine uprising that was later perverted by bureaucracy or Stalin
- **optimist**: imperial Russia was successfully modernising until the war ruined progress
- **pessimist**: Tsarist regime was inherently brittle and doomed regardless of the war
- **libertarian**: popular revolution hijacked by power-hungry Bolsheviks
- **revisionist**: focus on the high political awareness and agency of the masses

## Timeline
- **1861**: emancipation of the serfs (Alexander II)
- **1894**: Nicholas II becomes Tsar
- **1903**: split of social democrats into Bolsheviks and Mensheviks
- **1904&ndash;1905**: Russo-Japanese war
- **1905 (January)**: Bloody Sunday massacre
- **1905 (October)**: Tsar issues October Manifesto
- **1906**: meeting of the first Duma
- **1906&ndash;1911**: Peter Stolypin's era of land reform and repression
- **1911**: assassination of Stolypin
- **1914 (July)**: outbreak of the First World War
- **1917 (February)**: February revolution &rarr; abdication of the Tsar &rarr; dual power established
- **1917 (April)**: Lenin returns to Petrograd and issues the April Theses
- **1917 (July)**: July Days demonstrations in Petrograd
- **1917 (October)**: October revolution &rarr; Bolsheviks overthrow provisional government
- **1918&ndash;1920**: Russian civil war between reds and whites
- **1924 (January)**: death of Lenin
