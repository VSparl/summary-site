## Proximate and ultimate questions (35.1)

- **behaviour**: everything an animal does and how it does it
- interest because of hunting / being hunted of ancestors
- **proximate cause**: environmental stimulus that triggers a behaviour
- **ultimate cause**: the reason why this behavioural reaction exists at all

## Fixed action patterns (FAPs) (35.2)

- **innate behaviour**: behaviour that is the same for the entire species, encoded in their genes
- **FAP**: unchangeable behavioural sequence
- will usually be completed in its entirety, even if other stimuli are received
- examples:
    - **graylag goose**: egg retrieval will be finished even if it is taken away
    - **birds**: parents will push food into anything that has the same colouring as the open beaks from their offspring
- increases the fitness if e.g. the action has to work on the first try every time

## Behavior is the result of both genetic and environmental factors (35.3)

- behaviour can be genetically programmed (example: fruit flies)
    - _fruitless_ gene encodes for a protein only found in males
    - males without _fruitless_ gene are attracted to other males
    - females with _fruitless_ gene are attracted to other females
    - &rarr; _fruitless_ gene dictates whom the fly is attracted to
- behaviour can be influenced by environmental factors (example: Norway rats)
    - some mothers spend much time grooming / licking their offspring
    - some mother spend little time grooming / licking their offspring
    - the latter are more sensitive to fight-or-flight stimuli &rarr; become low-interaction mothers themselves
    - others are more relaxed and become high-interaction mothers
    - proven by **cross-fostering**-experiment: offspring from a low-interaction mother is placed with a high-interaction mother &rarr; they become high-interaction

## Learning (35.4 - 35.6, 35.9 – 35.11)

- adapt the behavioural responses to environmental changes
### imprinting

- limited to a specific time period in the animal's life
- example: graylag geese
    - offspring take the thing they see in their first few hours as their mother, even if it is a human or a plane
    - most important stimulus: walking away from the nest while making (any) sounds
- usecase:
    - to protect one's nest from different offspring
    - to follow the lead of your parent
    - to learn mating song / behaviour of your species
- issue with conservation prgrammes: humans can't help bird chicks to grow up, since they will become attached

### associative learning

- learn to associate one stimulus with a reward or punishment
- **trial-and-error learning**: when an animal behaves in a certain way, a positive or negative response is triggered which then teaches the animal (example: coyotes)
    - when trying to eat a porcupine, it attacks and sticks its spikes into the coyote's mouth
    - the coyote is hurt and therefore remembers not to attack porcupines anymore
- **social learning**: observe the behaviour of others (example: vervet monkeys)
    - when seeing leopards, eagles or snakes, they give off different alarm signals
    - depending on what signal the others hear, they either run up a tree (leopard), look up (eagle) or look down (snake)
    - the younger learn these signals from the older and get better over time
- **cognition**: use the nervous system receive, store, process and use information collected by sensory receptors (example: chimpanzees)
    - a chimpanzee is placed in a room with a banana hung from the ceiling and boxes lying around on the floor
    - after some time, the chimpanzee will start to stack the boxes in order to reach the banana

## Classical and operant conditioning (Pavlov/Skinner)

### classical conditioning

- unconditioned stimulus triggers an unconditioned response
- neutral stimulus triggers no conditioned response
- when conditioning, both stimuli are given and the unconditioned stimulus triggers the unconditioned response
- after conditioning, the previously neutral stimulus becomes a conditioned stimulus and triggers a conditioned response
- example: Pavlov's dog
    - food triggers salivation
    - tuning fork doesn't trigger any response
    - the dog is shown food with the tuning fork sound multiple times
    - now, the sound of the tuning fork triggers salivation

### operant conditioning

- modifies voluntary (operant) behaviour
- when an individual does a wanted behaviour, you reward it
- when an individual does an unwanted behaviour, you punish it
- example: Skinner box
    - rat has a lever that either triggers a reward or punishment (depending on experiment)
    - food pellet distribution box acts as reward
    - metal bars in the floor connected to a shock generator act as punishment
    - the rat can be trained to either push the lever purposely (reward) or avoid pressing it (punishment)
    - interesting phenomena can be watched when only triggering the mechanisms randomly if the lever is pressed

## Cuteness

- mostly infant-like physical traits
    - small body size
    - disproportionately large head
- triggers nurturing instinct in adults &rarr; ensures that the adults like their children no matter what
- some animals use that to their advantage: pedomorphosis (animals retain childlike traits even as adults)

## Foraging and mating behaviors (35.12 – 35.14)

- **generalists**: species that consume a wide range of different foods (e.g. seagull, boar)
- **specialists**: species that consume a niche range of foods (e.g. koala, )
- use a **search image** to find foods: if the current primary food becomes scarce, the animal may look for different foods
- **optimal foraging theory**: feeding behaviour should provide maximal energy gain with minimal energy expense and minimal risk of being eaten while foraging
- hunting in groups may improve success by lowering the energy expenditure

---

- needs of young are important for mating system
    - if offspring can't gather food itself, a single parent might not be enough
    - if offspring can immediately gather food themselves, one parent might be enough, the other can increase its fitness by producing more offspring
- **certainty of paternity** is important: only if the offspring you care for is yours, your genes are carried on
    - when fertilisation and "birth" happens at the same time, the males are more likely to stay (since they know it is their offspring)
    - if they happen further apart, the males are more likely to leave (since they don't know if it is their offspring)

---

- usecase of **courtship rituals**
    - identify that the partner is from the same species
    - make sure the partner has good genes
    - make sure that the male is dominant

## Mating behavior of elephant seals

- harems consisting of 25-50 females around one alpha bull
- usually surrounded by beta bulls (tolerated by alpha since they keep other bulls away) that hope to get a chance when alpha is busy
- alpha protects females from "attacks" by other bulls

## Sexy son hypothesis

- ideal partner choice of a female: male with genes that produces male offspring with the best chance of reproductive success &rarr; other advantages count less

## Social behavior; agonistic and altruistic behavior (35.15 - 35.18, 35.20 - 35.21)

### social behaviour

- any kind of interaction between animals, usually of the same species
- **territorial behaviour**:
    - used to feed, mate or rear offspring
    - often marked using scent markers
    - offers exclusive access to food, breeding areas and safe places to raise the young
- requires communication (transmission of signals) between individuals
    - sounds, touches, movements, chemical signals (smell / taste), visual signs

### agonistic behaviour

- a way of settling disputes between individuals, without actually fighting
- impress the other, perform a test of strength, etc. until one becomes submissive
- better, since by fighting both may become injured and thus less fit
- technique used to keep dominance hierarchies stable within groups
    - when a single (alpha) individual decides, the others don't waste energy / time fighting for it &rarr; good for fitness of species

### altruistic behaviour

- most behaviour types are selfish (only there to increase the own fitness)
- **altruism**: reduce one's fitness in order to increase the fitness of others (e.g. warning other individuals from a danger by shouting and thus exposing oneself)
- still good for one's fitness: when protecting your relatives, they will carry on genes similar / equal to yours &rarr; **kin selection**
- **reciprocal altruism**: rare behaviour, when you protect a non-relative, hoping that the favour might be paid back later

## Human behavior (35.23)

- experiments with twins, since they have the same genes
- genetic differences are responsible for about 50% of all variation between different humans

## The Milgram experiment

- experimenter, teacher and learner
- experimenter leads the experiment
- teacher believes he is just assisting, but actually he is the subject
- learner is an actor and assists the experimenter
- teacher asks questions, and for wrong answers, gives them a (fake) electric shock, with an acted reaction from the learner
- if the teacher refuses, the experimenter will request him to continue
- at one point, they stop and tell what is going on
- goal of the experiment: learn about obedience to authority, even if it goes against the morale
- couldn't be performed anymore today, goes against ethics
